D. Anecdote

Ms. B heard about assistive technology (AT) services through the Tennessee Technology Access Program (TTAP) partnering AT center by word of mouth. Ms. B stopped by to inquire if the AT center could assist her mother who sustained a severe stroke. As the caregiver for her mother, Ms. B struggles to transfer her from one area of the home to another. The duties of caregiving were causing fatigue and aggravating an existing neck injury resulting in significant pain. Ms. B’s doctor warned that continuing to care for her mother in this way would lead to a permanent injury of her own. A team member of the reuse program provided the Ms. B with a hospital bed and mattress, a manual wheelchair, a Hoyer lift and a sliding board for her mother. After a few days, she called back to thank staff for blessing her mother with AT and durable medical equipment. She also reported she anticipates she’ll slowly recover from her neck injury now that she doesn’t have to physically lift and move her mother any longer. The reuse program was a life saver to the mother and daughter, relieving the stress of lifting her mom and no longer risking her own health. The availability of these items in the reuse program also saved Ms. B the expense of purchasing these items, which can be very difficult on limited funds.

E. Anecdote

K is a 15-year old girl with physical and speech limitations. K previously attended “ChatterCamp”, the augmentative and alternative communication (AAC) camp hosted by the Tennessee Technology Access Program partnering assistive technology (AT) center in southeast Tennessee. While attending the camp this year, she expressed an interest in photography. Her parents were interested in learning about technology options that might allow her to explore photography, and in determining if photography was a genuine interest before investing in adaptive equipment. Access to assistive technology loaner made this possible. The rehab engineer assisted K in exploring devices available to enable her to take photographs. K was then loaned an iPod Touch, a bluetooth wireless switch interface, and a mount used to attach this equipment to her wheelchair. K used her head to operate the switch interface controlling the iPod Touch. With this loaner equipment, she took photographs while exploring elements of photography and composition. She used her electric wheelchair controls to position/tilt her chair (and thus the attached iPod) so that the photo subject was “in frame”. She then pressed the head switch to take pictures with the iPod. The loan assisted K and her parents in making the decision to purchase the equipment for K. She borrows the new equipment until her own equipment purchased by her parents.

D. Anecdote

The assistive technology (AT) demonstration program provided Ms. C the opportunity to identify a device to allow her to continue doing what she loves. Ms. C’s did not want to give up her beloved hobbies of sewing and embroidery as her eyesight began to deteriorate as a result of Multiple Sclerosis. She could no longer see well enough to thread the sewing machine. While self-threading sewing machines are available, they do not have the desired embroidery features. Ms. C went to the Tennessee Technology Access Program (TTAP) AT center in southwest Tennessee hoping to learn about options to magnify her needles and thread so she could load and operate her sewing machine independently. She tried a variety of handheld magnifiers, none of which worked for her because she needed both hands free for the needle and thread. After trying handheld devices, the rehabilitation engineer tried a head-mounted magnifier system. These devices include a camera that projects a magnified image onto small screens positioned in front of the eyes. This option showed a lot promise for her. To ensure she identified a workable solution, staff at the AT center went with her to the sewing machine store to try different models of head-mounted digital magnifiers. With a little practice, these devices enabled Ms. C to thread a sewing machine needle all by herself! The AT center staff put her in contact with the vendor to purchase the device. She was very pleased as to know she can continue enjoying her hobbies independently.

B. Description of Training Activities

Describe innovative one high-impact assistance training activity conducted during the reporting period:

In collaboration with the assistive technology (AT) center located in west Tennessee, the Tennessee Technology Access Program (TTAP) provides training twice per week to military veteran dependents with intellectual disabilities. The weekly trainings consist of various topics, meeting the need of participants. The focus of the most recent quarter included computer activities to help identify safety/warning signs in office buildings, using assistive technology devices to improve independence with dressing, and the continued to address literacy skills. Training sessions assist individuals who often have no other resources to acquire such supports and develops skills necessary for enjoying more independent lives.

Breifly describe one training activity related to transition conducted during the reporting period:

Partnerships make services happen and promote success! The Tennessee Technology Access Program and collaborating assistive technology (AT) center in southeast Tennessee partnered with “Tech Goes Home (TGH), a national non-profit focused on bridging the digital divide. In a world where access to technology is required for success, many low-income communities lack this access placing them at an even greater disadvantage. Tech Goes Home provides tablets to low-income families, provided the participants complete 15 hours of focused instruction on how to use the devices effectively for tasks besides entertainment. The series of trainings addressed different scenarios of transition. In the second quarter of the fiscal year, the AT program and TGH worked with seven families of children with disabilities who were primarily transitioning out of a special-needs Pre-K environment into a public school. Tech Goes Home provided the families with Apple iPad mini tablets. Assistive technology staff provided training on how to use the iPad (basic features, creating an AppleID, creating a Gmail account, using Google Docs, researching online, basic Internet etiquette, etc.) The assistive technology services staff also provided training on the accessibility features on the iPad, such as a low-vision accommodations, switch control, and Guided Access (a special mode that locks children out of more advanced features). The TGH AT staff partnered with the special-education teachers at the AT center to demonstrate apps for different disabilities and how to use these apps with the children. In the third quarter of the fiscal year, the focus progressed to training several low-income families of rising kindergartners on Android tablets provided by TGH. AT center Staff provided 7.5 hours of training on using the devices both as tool for the adults as well as educational uses for the children. Part of TGH’s mission is “train the trainer”, so some of the staff members at the school were trained as well. This ongoing partnership enables families to have access to AT who would not otherwise have AT and to transition successfully.

Breifly describe one training activity related to Information and Communication Technology accessibility:

One way the Tennessee Technology Access Program’s (TTAP) partnering assistive technology (AT) center in west Tennessee addresses website accessibility for consumers with disabilities is through a YouTube video channel created by technical outreach staff. The center created accessibility modules recently used when facilitating training for specific members of a company creating online forms and website materials. The company’s previous forms and websites were not accessible, preventing access to individuals with vision loss utilizing screen readers to access the information. The outreach staff is readily available for follow-up trainings as well as technical support. The company who received training responded as follows: "The online YouTube channel that you created to provide free training on accessibility to multimedia software, development of accessible documents and accessing different websites was awesome. After meeting with you all, we are excited about being able to access the training when we have a question or need to train new employees who create documents for our websites and online materials. Kudos! Thanks for continuously adding to your samples of resources on how to make .pdf files accessible. After receiving our initial training from you all, we were able to show our person who creates .pdf files for our company. We want to make sure that people who are blind can access our documents.”
